Best Prop Firms for Trading Indices (US30, NAS100, SPX500)

Published 2026-03-09 · 8 min read

Add photo credit here once you choose an image (e.g. “Photo by Jane Doe on Unsplash”)

Major equity indices — commonly traded under tickers like US30, NAS100 and SPX500 — are among the most popular instruments on funded accounts because of their liquidity, clear intraday structure, and familiarity to traders coming from both CFD and futures backgrounds. Choosing a firm for index trading specifically means looking past the general marketing and checking a few index-specific details.

What actually matters for index traders

  • Spread and commission structure during high-volatility windows — index spreads can widen sharply around the open of major sessions or scheduled news.
  • News-trading restrictions — some firms restrict or void trades held through high-impact news events, which matters enormously for index traders since indices are highly reactive to macro data.
  • Session-specific liquidity — index CFDs and futures both see the deepest liquidity around their home exchange's cash session; confirm the firm doesn't restrict trading during the specific session your strategy depends on.
  • Overnight/rollover handling — index CFD positions typically carry a daily rollover cost or credit, which affects multi-day index strategies differently than intraday ones.

CFD indices vs index futures

AspectIndex CFDs (e.g. via MT4/MT5)Index futures (e.g. E-mini S&P 500)
Contract standardizationBroker-defined, can vary between firmsExchange-standardized tick value
Typical trading hoursNear-continuous with a daily close/reopenDefined session with a maintenance break
Cost structureSpread plus overnight financingCommission plus exchange fees

News-trading rules deserve special attention

Index prices react sharply to central bank decisions, employment data and inflation releases. Some funded-account rules explicitly restrict opening new positions in a window around these releases, or require positions to be flat before the announcement. Confirm this rule specifically before building an index strategy around trading through, rather than around, major news — a strategy that depends on news volatility can be structurally incompatible with a firm that restricts it.

Practical check: before funding an index-focused evaluation, open the firm's news-trading policy and specifically check whether it applies to CFDs, futures, or both — the wording sometimes differs between instrument categories within the same firm.

Sizing considerations specific to indices

Index CFDs and futures both carry a meaningfully higher point value than most forex pairs, which means a small miscalculation in lot or contract size translates into a larger swing against the daily loss limit than the same error would on a currency pair. Recalculating position size specifically for the index's point value — rather than reusing a forex sizing habit — is one of the most common adjustments index traders need to make when moving to a funded account.

Are indices more heavily restricted than forex pairs on funded accounts?
Not universally, but news-trading restrictions are more consequential for indices given how reactive they are to scheduled economic data.
Do overnight index positions cost more than forex overnight positions?
Overnight financing varies by instrument and broker; check the specific swap or financing rate for the index you trade rather than assuming it mirrors forex costs.
Ticker names (US30, NAS100, SPX500) refer to commonly used market symbols for major indices and are used here for general educational reference only. Contract specifications and rules vary by firm and broker.
