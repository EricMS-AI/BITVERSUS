FTMO vs Topstep: Which Prop Firm Is Better for You?

Published 2026-01-19 · 8 min read

FTMO and Topstep are two of the longest-running names in the funded-account space, but they were built around two different markets: FTMO around forex and CFDs, Topstep around futures. Comparing them head to head is less about "which is better" in the abstract and more about which market and rule structure fits how you already trade.

Core structural differences

AspectFTMO-style modelTopstep-style model
Primary marketForex, indices, commodities, crypto CFDsFutures (equity index, energy, metals, rates)
Typical platformMT4/MT5, cTraderRithmic-based platforms, NinjaTrader, TradingView
Evaluation structureOne or two-step challenge with a profit target per phaseCombine-style evaluation with a trailing drawdown
Weekend exposureOften allowed with some restrictionsNot applicable — futures markets close
Typical minimum trading daysAround 4–10 days per phaseVaries by combine, commonly a set number of profitable days

Why the "better" firm depends on your market

If your entire trading history is on forex majors and gold, a futures-first evaluation forces you to learn a new instrument, new tick values and a different session structure at the same time you are trying to pass an evaluation — not an efficient combination. The reverse is equally true for a futures scalper being asked to adapt to spread-based forex pricing overnight.

Drawdown mechanics: the detail that decides pass rates

Both models use a maximum loss limit, but the way it trails your account balance differs. A trailing drawdown that locks in gains as your equity rises rewards traders who protect profits early; a static drawdown calculated from the starting balance gives more room after an early gain but can feel less forgiving on day one. Neither is objectively superior — they simply reward different trading rhythms, which is why reading the specific rules document matters more than the brand name.

Cost and account scaling

Entry prices in both ecosystems scale with account size, and both offer scaling plans that increase buying power after a run of consistent, rule-compliant months. The practical question to ask is how quickly a scaling plan actually increases your allocated capital, and whether it requires maintaining a minimum balance that could itself be affected by a single rough week.

Neither model is inherently "easier." Traders often assume the cheaper or more marketed option is the safer bet, but pass rates are driven far more by whether the evaluation matches your existing skill set than by which company runs it.

A simple decision framework

  • If your edge is in forex, indices as CFDs, or gold with flexible session timing — an FTMO-style evaluation lets you keep trading the way you already do.
  • If your edge is in futures scalping or day-session index futures — a Topstep-style combine avoids forcing you into an unfamiliar CFD pricing model.
  • If you are undecided, a smaller, cheaper evaluation in each model is a low-cost way to find out which rule structure actually suits your trading rhythm before committing to a larger account.
Can I run both types of accounts at once?
Yes, many funded traders hold both forex/CFD and futures funded accounts simultaneously to diversify income across markets and firms.
Does one model have better payout speed?
Payout speed depends more on the specific firm's current operations than on the forex-vs-futures model itself; check recent, independently verifiable payout reports for the firm you're considering.
