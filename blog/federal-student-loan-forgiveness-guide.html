Federal Student Loan Forgiveness: The Ultimate Guide to PSLF and Income-Driven Plans

A plain-English breakdown of how forgiveness actually works in 2026 — including the Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) rules, the phase-out of SAVE, PAYE, and ICR, the new Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP), and the return of federal taxes on forgiven balances.

Forgiveness 12 min read Last updated: July 2026 Reviewed for accuracy against studentaid.gov

Federal student loan forgiveness is not one program — it is a collection of different pathways, each with its own eligibility rules, timelines, and tax consequences. That distinction matters more than ever in 2026, after a wave of legislative and regulatory changes reshaped almost every forgiveness route available to federal borrowers. This guide walks through exactly how forgiveness works today, what changed, and how to avoid the mistakes that most often disqualify borrowers.

Why this matters right now: Between mid-2025 and mid-2026, the Department of Education phased out the SAVE Plan, launched a new income-driven repayment option called the Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP), updated PSLF employer eligibility rules, and allowed a federal tax exemption on forgiven balances to expire. If the last thing you read about student loan forgiveness was written before 2025, it is almost certainly out of date.

How Student Loan Forgiveness Works in 2026

At a high level, federal student loan forgiveness falls into three broad categories:

  • Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) — full forgiveness after 10 years of qualifying payments while working for a government or eligible nonprofit employer.
  • Income-driven repayment (IDR) forgiveness — any remaining balance is cancelled after 20–30 years of payments calculated as a percentage of income, depending on the plan.
  • Discharge-based forgiveness — for specific circumstances such as total and permanent disability, school closure, or borrower defense to repayment (when a school misled you).

Each path has its own clock, its own paperwork, and — as of this year — its own tax treatment. Confusing them is one of the most common (and costly) mistakes borrowers make.

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF): The Complete Rules

PSLF remains the fastest forgiveness path available, but it is also the most procedurally demanding. To qualify, a borrower generally needs all of the following:

  • Eligible loans: Direct Loans only. Older FFEL or Perkins loans must first be consolidated into a Direct Consolidation Loan.
  • Qualifying employment: Full-time work (30+ hours a week, or your employer's definition of full-time) for a U.S. federal, state, local, or tribal government agency, or a 501(c)(3) nonprofit.
  • Qualifying repayment plan: Payments must be made under an income-driven plan or another qualifying plan — payments made under the Standard 10-year plan generally count too, though the loan would already be paid off by year ten in most cases.
  • 120 qualifying payments: They do not need to be consecutive, but they must be made while working for a qualifying employer.
  • A submitted PSLF application: Forgiveness is not automatic. Borrowers must file the PSLF form and have their employment certified, ideally on an annual basis rather than waiting until year ten.

Employer eligibility is changing. A Department of Education final rule gives the Secretary of Education new authority to disqualify employers found to have a "substantial illegal purpose." The rule does not change who is generally eligible (government agencies and 501(c)(3) nonprofits) and does not apply retroactively to work performed before its effective date. Because this rule has faced legal challenges and its status can shift quickly, borrowers should verify current employer eligibility directly on studentaid.gov before making career decisions based on PSLF.

The PSLF Buyback Program

If you had months in forbearance, deferment, or on a non-qualifying plan while working for a qualifying employer, the PSLF Buyback program may let you retroactively "buy back" that time by making a lump-sum payment equal to what you would have paid under an income-driven plan. This is especially useful for borrowers who are one or two payments away from the 120-payment threshold.

Income-Driven Repayment Forgiveness After the 2025–2026 Overhaul

This is where the biggest changes have landed. For years, borrowers could choose between several income-driven repayment (IDR) plans — SAVE, PAYE, IBR, and ICR — each forgiving remaining balances after 20 or 25 years. Legislation enacted in mid-2025 restructured this system substantially:

  • The SAVE Plan has been wound down and is no longer a long-term enrollment option.
  • Borrowers who took out their first federal loan before July 1, 2026 can generally remain on the Standard, Graduated, Extended, or current Income-Based Repayment (IBR) plan, or choose to enroll in the new RAP plan, through a transition window running to July 1, 2028.
  • Borrowers who do not actively choose a plan by that deadline will be defaulted into either IBR or RAP.
  • Anyone taking out a new federal loan on or after July 1, 2026 has only two repayment options going forward: the Tiered Standard Plan or RAP. PAYE, ICR, and new SAVE enrollment are no longer available to these borrowers.

In practice, this means the menu of income-driven options is shrinking sharply for anyone borrowing after mid-2026, while existing borrowers get a multi-year window to plan their move.

The Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP), Explained

RAP is the new default income-driven plan and the only IDR option for most new borrowers. Its mechanics differ meaningfully from the plans it replaces:

  • Payment formula: A percentage of adjusted gross income (AGI), tiered from roughly 1% to 10% depending on income, divided by 12 for a monthly amount.
  • Dependent credit: The monthly payment is reduced by $50 for each dependent claimed on your tax return.
  • Minimum payment: $10 a month, even for borrowers with little or no taxable income.
  • Interest handling: Unpaid interest on full, on-time payments is waived, and the Department adds a small matching principal payment when a borrower's reduction falls short of $50 a month — a feature intended to keep balances from growing indefinitely.
  • Forgiveness timeline: Any remaining balance is forgiven after 360 qualifying payments — 30 years — considerably longer than the 20–25 year timelines under the legacy plans it replaces.
  • Eligibility gap: Parent PLUS loans are not eligible for RAP unless first consolidated into a Direct Consolidation Loan, and even then, treatment differs from standard Direct Loans.

RAP's interest subsidy can meaningfully slow balance growth for lower-income borrowers, but the 30-year horizon means it is not a fast path to forgiveness for most people — it is best understood as a payment-affordability plan first and a forgiveness plan second.

Is Forgiven Debt Taxable? The 2026 "Tax Bomb"

This is the single most important update for borrowers approaching forgiveness in 2026 or later.

Tax treatment update

PSLF discharge remains tax-free at the federal level — this is a permanent statutory exclusion and was not affected by recent changes.

IDR forgiveness (including RAP, legacy IBR, PAYE, ICR, and SAVE) is taxable again as of January 1, 2026. A temporary federal tax exemption on forgiven balances, first enacted in 2021, expired at the end of 2025 and was not renewed.

In practice, that means a borrower whose remaining balance is cancelled through an income-driven plan will receive a Form 1099-C and must report the forgiven amount as taxable income for that year — potentially pushing them into a higher tax bracket and generating a five-figure tax bill. Borrowers who expect IDR forgiveness in the coming years should start setting aside funds well in advance rather than being surprised at tax time.

Other Federal Discharge Options

Beyond PSLF and IDR forgiveness, a smaller number of borrowers qualify for discharge through other channels:

  • Total and Permanent Disability (TPD) Discharge — for borrowers who can document a qualifying disability through the SSA, VA, or a physician's certification.
  • Closed School Discharge — for borrowers whose school closed while they were enrolled or shortly after they withdrew.
  • Borrower Defense to Repayment — for borrowers who can show their school misrepresented material facts, such as job placement rates or accreditation status.
  • Death Discharge — federal loans are discharged upon the borrower's death and are not passed on to a co-signer, except in certain Parent PLUS scenarios.

Step-by-Step: How to Pursue PSLF Correctly

  1. Confirm your loans are Direct Loans. If not, consolidate — but check the impact on your qualifying payment count first.
  2. Submit the PSLF form annually, not just at the end, so your employment and payment count are certified in real time and errors get caught early.
  3. Stay on a qualifying repayment plan. If your loans transition out of a legacy IDR plan, choose the qualifying option deliberately rather than letting a default assignment happen.
  4. Track your qualifying payment count through your servicer's PSLF tracker and studentaid.gov.
  5. Review the PSLF Buyback program if you have gaps caused by forbearance, deferment, or a non-qualifying plan.
  6. File your final PSLF application once you hit 120 qualifying payments — forgiveness is not automatic.

Key Takeaways

  • PSLF still offers full forgiveness after 120 qualifying payments and remains federally tax-free — but employer eligibility rules are evolving, so verify your employer's status directly with studentaid.gov.
  • SAVE, PAYE, and ICR are being phased out. Existing borrowers have until July 1, 2028 to choose a new qualifying plan before being defaulted into one.
  • New borrowers after July 1, 2026 can only choose between the Tiered Standard Plan and RAP.
  • RAP offers a $10 minimum payment, dependent credits, and an interest subsidy — but stretches forgiveness out to 30 years.
  • IDR forgiveness is taxable again starting in 2026; PSLF forgiveness is not. Plan your finances accordingly.
